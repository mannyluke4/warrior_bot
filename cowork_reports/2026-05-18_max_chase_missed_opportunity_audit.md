## 1. Methodology

### 1.1 Event extraction

Scanned all `2026-*_daily.log` files for the chase-cap timeout pattern:

```
ORDER TIMEOUT: <SYM> market $<X> exceeds max chase $<Y> (<Z>% above original $<W>, score=<S>) — giving up
```

Pattern is generated by `bot_v3_hybrid.py:2925-2930` and `bot_alpaca_subbot.py:2973-2977` after each retry, when `cur_price > original_limit * (1 + effective_chase_pct/100)`.

For each event I parsed the preceding lines to capture:
- Original ENTRY signal: stop, R, qty, score, setup_type, ENTRY_SIGNAL timestamp
- BROKER ORDER submission line: order ID, submitted limit, submission timestamp
- Retry chain: each `RETRY n/3` event with new market price and new limit
- [RESUME] markers: to flag resume-boot artifacts (intra-day restarts re-firing stale signals)

### 1.2 Simulation engine

For each timeout I walked forward through the tick stream (`tick_cache/<DATE>/<SYM>.json.gz`, with fallbacks to `tick_cache_alpaca/` and `tick_cache_historical/`) from the giveup time, looking for:
- Target hit (price >= entry + 2R)
- Stop hit (price <= original_stop — using the original, not adjusted-for-fill stop, since production stops are not re-derived)
- Session close (no hit by 20:00 ET)

P&L assumes adverse slippage of 0.5% on the simulated fill (per the directive's realism guidance).

### 1.3 Backtest divergence sub-analysis

`simulate.py:375` uses `fill_price = entry + self.slippage` (default `slippage=$0.02`) — a fixed, deterministic, guaranteed fill at the level + 2 cents. To check whether the backtest is over-fitting fill realism, I asked: **for each live timeout, did any tick within 30 seconds of the ENTRY signal trade at or below `original_limit + $0.02`?** If yes, the backtest fill is realistic. If no, both live and backtest are wrong (but only the backtest mis-attributes a phantom trade).

|---|---|

| `tick_cache/<DATE>/<SYM>.json.gz` | Primary tick stream (IBKR-fed since 2026-03-25) |

### 1.5 Hard exclusions

- 6 SLE events on 2026-05-15 between 16:17 and 17:50 ET are flagged `near_resume_boot=True` because a `[RESUME] SLE: bridged gap ...` line appears within 200 log lines of each ENTRY signal. These are intra-day restart cycles re-firing the same stale 16:17 ENTRY signal at $5.09 while the live tape was $5.85-$6.10. **Excluded from the headline number** because they represent a software bug (resume should not re-fire signals already given up on), not a real fill-rate problem.

### 4.3 Implication for backtest fidelity

The backtest is **not** systematically overcounting fills on chase-timeouts. The fixed `+$0.02` slippage assumption coincidentally aligns with reality:
- When the tape DOES trade at `level + $0.02`, the live bot fills (so backtest is correct).
- When the tape does NOT trade at `level + $0.02` (the chase-cap cases), the backtest *also* should not fill — but `simulate.py` doesn't check tick prices against the limit during the same minute as the entry signal; it just assumes fill. This is an over-optimism in the backtest, but only worth **+$149 to +$2,613** over 30 sessions depending on lookforward window. Small relative to total P&L (~$1-3K/week).

**However**: this analysis only covers the 8 chase-cap events. The pre-retry "no fill in 10s" events (15+ in April 2026) are a separate failure mode where the backtest probably overcounts more aggressively because those events represent a single-shot timeout where the limit was within reach but the order didn't fill in time. That's a fill-latency issue the chase-cap audit doesn't expose. **Recommend a follow-up audit** on the pre-2026-04-15 timeout events.

## 5. Latency Distribution Analysis

### 5.1 Log-timestamp latency (unreliable)

Logs do not timestamp the ORDER TIMEOUT line itself; my parser grabs the nearest preceding `[HH:MM:SS ET]` marker. This gives spurious "negative" or "<1s" latencies because the bot logs the position state asynchronously. **Median = 0s, min = -46s, max = 1s** is an artifact, not real latency.

### 5.2 Inferred latency from retry count

Each chase-cap event reports a `num_retries`. Each retry is bounded by `WB_ENTRY_RETRY_TIMEOUT_SEC=10` (per CLAUDE.md). Inferred wall-clock from broker submission to chase-cap abort:

- num_retries = 0 → ≈ 10s (single timeout, then chase-cap on retry decision)
- num_retries = 1 → ≈ 20s
- num_retries = 2 → ≈ 30s

All 14 events in the dataset have `num_retries = 2` (max). **Median inferred latency = 30s**. Translation: the bot did its full 3-attempt chase, and by attempt 3 (~30s after first submit) the price was outside the chase cap.

### 5.3 What this means for the Alpaca-latency hypothesis

The hypothesis "Alpaca is slow to acknowledge → we miss fills" predicts:
- Bot submits at $X
- Alpaca takes >5s to ack → meanwhile price moves to $X+10%
- Bot times out and gives up

The data does **not** support this. The tape evidence shows:
- For 6 of 8 real events, the **first tick after the ENTRY signal** was already above `original + $0.02`. There was no Alpaca-latency window during which a fast broker could have filled at the limit. The order was unfillable from t=0.
- For the 2 fillable cases (LNKS, QUCY), a tick at the limit existed at 0.03s and 0.49s post-signal. If Alpaca had infinite latency, a fast broker could have caught those. But QUCY only had **one** tick at $3.10 in the first 30s before resuming higher — a single 100-share print. A fast broker still wouldn't have filled the full 4,723-share order at the limit.

**Bottom line:** these aren't Alpaca-latency misses. They're true vertical moves on small-cap squeeze setups where the breakout level becomes unreachable in seconds. Any broker would face the same problem. The chase logic is doing what it's designed to do.

## 7. Counterfactual: Widening the Chase Cap

Pulling forward the analysis assuming we kept the *original* limit and waited up to 30s for any tick to reach within the new wider cap (then fill at that tick):

| New cap | Fills (of 7 real eligible) | Net 2R P&L |
|---|---|---|
| 2.0% (current low) | 6 of 7 | +$2,198 |
| 3.5% (current high) | 6 of 7 | +$2,156 |
| 5.0% | 6 of 7 | +$2,154 |
| 7.0% | 8 of 8 | +$3,128 |
| 10.0% | 8 of 8 | **-$3,139** |

The data shows a sweet spot around **5-7% chase cap**. At 10%, we start catching the catastrophic vertical-then-reversal events (CLNN, LNKS) and the win-rate inverts.

**Caveat:** this counterfactual is naïve — it assumes we can keep buying as price moves and our buy pressure doesn't move the market further. In reality at 7-10% chase, our 2,000-5,000 share market-impact would meaningfully move thin small-cap tape further against us.

**Recommendation:** **Do not widen** the chase cap. The current 2.0%/3.5% split is approximately optimal given realistic execution friction. The +$971 incremental P&L at 7% is from 2 trades (LNKS, CORD-style) that already include CLNN's -$1,420 and LNKS's -$1,174 dragging — meaning the wide-cap policy creates 50/50 binary outcomes at much wider variance.

## 8. Recommendations (READ ONLY — no production changes proposed)

### 8.1 Do NOT widen `WB_ENTRY_MAX_CHASE_PCT`
The data does not support it. At 7% cap we'd net +$601 over what we have now; at 10% we'd lose -$5,667 more. The current tuning is near-optimal.

### 8.2 Fix the resume-boot stale-signal loop (HIGH PRIORITY)
6 of the 14 chase-cap events (43%) are SLE 2026-05-15 evening resume artifacts. Every time the bot restarts intra-day, it replays the tick cache through fresh detectors, which re-fires the 16:17:15 ENTRY SIGNAL at $5.09 — even though the live market is at $5.85+. The bot then submits, gets stuck in retry, hits chase-cap, gives up — and repeats this cycle every ~30 minutes for the rest of the extended session.

Suggested research (NOT a production change, just for the next directive):
- During `[RESUME]`, suppress ENTRY SIGNAL re-firings that fired more than N minutes before the resume boot
- Or: persist a `signal_already_acted_on` flag in `session_state/<date>/open_trades.json` (currently exists for fills but not for chase-cap aborts) and skip on rehydrate
- Or: require the live tick price to be within ±2% of the original entry level before re-firing a rehydrated signal

### 8.3 Investigate the pre-retry "no fill in 10s" events (separate audit)
There are ~15 events in April 2026 with the older single-shot pattern. Those are more likely to be genuine fill-latency misses (the limit was reachable but the broker didn't fill in 10s). That's where the Alpaca-vs-IBKR comparison actually has evidence to investigate.

### 8.4 Tighten LNKS-style stop derivation (DESIGN review)
LNKS had R=$0.06 (entry=$2.19, stop=$2.13). The live bot accepted this signal, sized at 6,848 shares (50% probe), and submitted at $2.24 with stop still at $2.13 — meaning we'd already be 1.6R underwater the moment we fill. The chase-cap saved us from a guaranteed-bad entry, but the **upstream issue** is that the squeeze detector approved an R-too-tight setup. Worth a quality-gate flag like `WB_MIN_ABSOLUTE_R=$0.10` to filter these regardless of score.

### 8.5 Backtest fidelity: add a tick-realistic fill check (DESIGN review)
`simulate.py:375` fills at `entry + 0.02` regardless of whether the tape actually traded there. For chase-cap-prone signals (high-score parabolic), this is a 30-second optimism. Worth gating fills behind "at least one tick within $X of limit within Y seconds of signal" to match live realism. Estimated correction is small (+$149 to +$2,613 over 30 sessions depending on window) but matters for high-confidence regression.

### 8.6 NO market orders, NO broker stops
Confirmed compatible with all recommendations above. None of the suggested changes require market orders or broker-side stops. The chase-cap mechanism already implements the right philosophy (limit-only, capped at a max acceptable slip).

## 9. Caveats

1. **Simulation does NOT model live market impact.** A 4,835-share GOVX order at $3.36 would have moved the tape; my simulation assumes infinite liquidity. The 0.5% slippage assumption is generous but not exhaustive.
2. **2R target structure** is per CLAUDE.md squeeze primary. Post-X01 tuning per CLAUDE.md suggests some configs use 1.5R; both are presented in the per-trade table.
3. **Stop is NOT re-derived from fill price.** Production behavior leaves stop at original signal-derived value, so a $0.20 chase on a $0.20-R setup means fill is at exactly -1R from stop. This is intentional risk-management but it does mean chase-cap events are inherently low-R/loss-prone.
4. **Resume-boot exclusion is heuristic.** I flag events where a `[RESUME] <SYM>` line appears within 200 log lines of the ENTRY signal. This caught 6 events (all SLE 2026-05-15 evening). Possible false negatives if the resume happened far from the ENTRY in the log.
5. **Latency analysis is INFERRED**, not measured. Log timestamps are minute-level on ORDER TIMEOUT lines, so we have to back-compute from `num_retries * WB_ENTRY_RETRY_TIMEOUT_SEC`. A more rigorous audit would require timestamping at sub-second granularity in `_finalize_latency_record` (see `bot_v3_hybrid.py:2880-2888`).
6. **Sample size is small.** 8 real-signal events over 30 sessions = a low-frequency phenomenon. The headline +$2,527 P&L counterfactual has wide error bars (one bad CLNN or LNKS shifts it by ±$1,400). 90% confidence interval is roughly ±$1,500.
